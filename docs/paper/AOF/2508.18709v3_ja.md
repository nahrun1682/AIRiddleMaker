# Adaptive Originality Filtering 論文 日本語訳（2508.18709v3）

- 原題: *Adaptive Originality Filtering: Rejection-Based Prompting and RiddleScore for Culturally Grounded Multilingual Riddle Generation*
- 著者: Duy Le, Kent Ziti, Evan Girard-Sun, Bakr Bouhaya, Sean O’Brien, Vasu Sharma, Kevin Zhu
- 所属: Algoverse AI Research
- 版: arXiv:2508.18709v3 (2025-10-09)

## 要旨（Abstract）
言語モデルは、多言語かつ創造的な生成能力を問われる機会が増えている。そこでは文化的文脈に根ざした抽象表現が求められるが、標準的なプロンプト手法は反復的・浅薄な出力になりやすい。本研究は **Adaptive Originality Filtering（AOF）** を提案する。AOF は意味的な棄却（semantic rejection）により、新規性と文化的一貫性を担保するプロンプティング戦略である。品質評価のために、**RiddleScore**（新規性・多様性・流暢性・解答整合性を統合）も提案する。AOF は Distinct-2（日本語 0.915）を改善し、Self-BLEU（0.177）を低減し、RiddleScore を向上（アラビア語で最大 +57.1%）させた。人手評価でも流暢性・創造性・文化適合性の向上が確認された。一方で改善幅は言語により異なり、アラビア語では Distinct-2 より RiddleScore の伸びが大きく、日本語では両者の変化幅が近い。対象はなぞなぞだが、手法自体はより広い創造的生成タスクにも適用可能である。総じて、複合評価と意味フィルタを組み合わせることで、ファインチューニングなしでも文化的に豊かな生成へ到達できる。

## 1. はじめに（Introduction）
大規模言語モデル（LLM）は自然言語処理を大きく進展させたが、創造的かつ多言語の文脈における生成能力は十分に検証されておらず、性能も不安定である。なぞなぞ生成は特に難しく、単なる流暢性だけでなく、比喩的抽象化、文化的共鳴、意味的曖昧性が同時に必要になる。これらは学習コーパスで過小表現されがちであり、LLM を世界的な教育・創作基盤へ組み込む際の包摂性と表現力を制約する。

なぞなぞは、比喩・ミスリード・文脈依存記号の組合せを含むため、多言語創造性評価の有力なベンチマークである。しかし既存の Zero-shot / Few-shot / CoT は、特に意味距離が大きい言語や形態論が豊かな言語で、定型化や誤訳を生みやすい。さらに BLEU、Perplexity、BERTScore は、構造的新規性・修辞密度・文化適合性といった「なぞなぞ固有の品質」を十分に捉えられない。

本研究はこのギャップを埋めるために、コサイン類似度ベースの棄却機構で意味的新規性と語彙多様性を強制する **AOF** を提案する。AOF はデコードループに外部制約を注入し、冗長または文化的不整合な出力を除外する。併せて **RiddleScore** を導入し、
- Novelty（新規性）
- Diversity（多様性）
- Fluency（流暢性）
- Semantic Alignment（意味整合）
の 4 軸を統合して評価する。

評価対象は GPT-4o、LLaMA 3.1、DeepSeek R1、言語は英語・中国語・アラビア語・日本語・フランス語。BiRdQA 上で同一デコード条件のもと、Self-BLEU / Distinct-2 / Cross-lingual BERTScore / 人手評価で比較した。結果として AOF は自動評価・人手評価の双方でベースラインを上回る。特に日本語では GPT-4o + AOF が Self-BLEU 0.177、Distinct-2 0.915 を示し、反復抑制と語彙多様化が顕著だった。

研究質問（RQ）は以下。
- RQ1: 棄却ベース・プロンプティング（AOF）は、言語類型が異なる多言語間で意味的新規性・語彙多様性を高められるか。
- RQ2: 提案複合指標 RiddleScore は、均等重みベースラインより人手評価と高く相関するか。
- RQ3: 事前学習モデルとファインチューニング済みモデルでは、AOF の効果はどう異なるか。

著者らの回答は次の通り。
- RQ1: θ=0.75 の AOF は多言語で有効。日本語で Self-BLEU 0.177（-63.4%）、Distinct-2 0.915。
- RQ2: RiddleScore は Spearman ρ=0.83 で、人間評価と強相関。
- RQ3: ファインチューニング済みモデルの方が AOF の恩恵が大きく、中国語で RiddleScore 0.453→0.728（+48.3%）、人手評価 3.91→4.50。

## 2. 関連研究（Related Work）
### 2.1 多言語・文化 NLP
既存研究の多くは「なぞなぞ生成」ではなく「理解・解答」に焦点がある。SemEval-2024 Task 9 や BiRdQA は有用な基盤だが、生成能力の本格評価には不足がある。多言語モデルは比喩・象徴表現に弱く、言語間埋め込み整列（LASER, XLM-R, MUSE）も詩的・修辞的文脈では崩れやすい。AOF は意味棄却と修辞フィルタで文化的流暢性を明示的に担保する。

### 2.2 創造的・比喩的生成
ジョーク、比喩、物語など創造タスクは「新規性」と「流暢性」のトレードオフを持つ。多様性を増す枠組みはあるが、意味制約や文化適応が弱い場合が多い。AOF は文化装置（比喩・慣用）を促す制約とリトライを組み合わせ、追加教師なしで新規性を高める点に特徴がある。

### 2.3 プロンプティングと制約付き生成
Few-shot/CoT は推論支援に有効な一方、学習済みテンプレート再生を助長しやすい。Self-Refine、Reflexion、ToT など反復改善系や COLD/Sketch-of-Thought など制約系手法もあるが、文化的新規性を明示的に保証しない。AOF は棄却サンプリング・比喩制約・言語間フィルタを単一ループに統合する。

### 2.4 多言語生成評価
BLEU/BERTScore は独創性や文化適合を捉えにくい。BLEURT/COMET/HUME 等にも限界があり、創造生成評価は依然難しい。RiddleScore は新規性・語彙多様性・流暢性・解答整合性を統合し、人手評価で妥当性を確認した。

## 3. 手法（Methodology）
### 3.1 Adaptive Originality Filtering（AOF）
AOF は次の 3 要素で構成される。
1. 意味類似フィルタ（MiniLM 埋め込み + コサイン類似度）
2. 棄却サンプリング（条件不達候補の再生成）
3. プロンプト制約（新規性・修辞性・文化適合を指示）

候補が参照集合と高類似なら棄却し、再生成する。しきい値は **θ=0.75**。閾値感度分析では、低すぎるとテンプレ流用が増え、高すぎると失敗率が 14% 上昇した。

### 3.2 RiddleScore
RiddleScore は以下の重み付き和。

\[
\text{RiddleScore}=\alpha\cdot\text{Novelty}+\beta\cdot\text{Diversity}+\gamma\cdot\text{Fluency}+\delta\cdot\text{Alignment}
\]

- Novelty: BiRdQA とのコサイン距離（MiniLM）
- Diversity: Distinct-2
- Fluency: 凍結 GPT-2.5 の逆 Perplexity
- Alignment: 解答との BERTScore

重みは \(\alpha=0.30,\beta=0.20,\gamma=0.30,\delta=0.20\)。120 サンプル開発セットのグリッド探索で、均等重みの ρ=0.71 から ρ=0.83 に改善。

### 3.3 実験設定
モデル: GPT-4o, LLaMA 3.1, DeepSeek R1

プロンプト: Zero-shot / Few-shot / CoT / Adversarial / AOF

共通デコード: temperature=0.7

データ: BiRdQA（約 15k）

評価: Self-BLEU, Distinct-2, Cross-lingual BERTScore, RiddleScore, 人手評価。構文妥当性は spaCy と Stanza で検証。

## 4. GPT-4o のファインチューニング
目的は、GPT-4o-2024-08-06 の多言語なぞなぞ「解答」と「生成」を強化すること。なぞなぞは単純一致でなく、比喩理解・論理逆説・ミスリード推論を要するため、正答率向上だけでなく構造的推論力の獲得を目指す。

問題設定は BiRdQA に基づく教師あり多クラス分類（四択）で、損失はクロスエントロピー。プロンプト戦略は事前学習モデル実験と同じ 5 種（Zero/Few/CoT/Adversarial/AOF）。比較対象は事前学習 GPT-4o、LLaMA 3.1、DeepSeek R1。

## 5. 人手評価（Human Evaluation）
評価軸は 4 つ。
- Fluency（流暢性）
- Novelty（新規性）
- Cultural Fit（文化適合）
- Answerability（解答可能性）

母語または高熟達の評価者が 1〜5 点 Likert で採点し、モデル名はブラインド化。

### 5.1 結果
AOF は事前学習・ファインチューニングの双方で、全言語平均の最高得点を示した。最高例はアラビア語 4.92、英中仏で 4.50。評価者コメントでは「詩的表現」「文化的アンカー」「構造整合」が高評価理由として挙がる。

RiddleScore と人手評価は概ね一致。AOF で高 RiddleScore の言語ほど人手改善が大きく、RiddleScore の妥当性を支持する。

## 6. AOF（事前学習モデル）評価
AOF はファインチューニングなしでも、全言語で比喩的新規性と構造流暢性を改善。DeepSeek R1 は複数言語で高 RiddleScore（例: EN 0.400, AR 0.400, ZH 0.453, JA 0.379）を示し、AOF の棄却ループと相性が良い。GPT-4o は流暢だが比喩的大胆さで劣る場面があり、LLaMA 3.1 は語彙多様性は高い一方で一貫性が不安定。

共通傾向として、AOF はテンプレ流用を抑え、自己中心表現を減らし、チューニングなしで文化適合を高める。

## 7. AOF（ファインチューニング後）評価
GPT-4o を AOF 併用でファインチューニングすると、EN/ZH/FR/AR で一貫して向上。
- RiddleScore 改善: +33.4%（AR）〜 +48.3%（ZH）
- Self-BLEU: 33〜51% 減
- Distinct-2: 6〜13% 増

中国語の例「千言万語蔵心懐」（答え: 信）は、文字構造・比喩圧縮・文化性を同時に満たす高品質例として示される。

## 8. 事前学習 vs. ファインチューニング
AOF 付きファインチューニングで、5 言語すべてに改善。
- AR +57.1%
- ZH +48.3%
- EN +43.4%
- FR +33.7%
- JA +29.5%

Self-BLEU は JA で最大 -63.4%、FR で -43.2%。Distinct-2 も AR/JA/FR で大きく改善。人手評価も多くの言語で上がり、RiddleScore の変化と整合した。

Few-shot と AOF は比較的一貫して向上するが、CoT は言語依存性が高く安定しない。全言語で安定して自動・人手指標を改善したのは AOF。

## 9. 実世界なぞなぞとの比較
AOF（微調整後）は、実世界の定型（二項対立、韻、音韻遊び）より、概念ブレンディング・間接比喩・領域横断抽象を多用する傾向がある。英仏では反響・影・深度メタファ、中文日文では字形推論や空間逆説、アラビア語では象徴層の重ね合わせが強まる。総じて、定型性を減らし、語彙創意と文化深度を増す。

## 10. 結論
AOF は、多言語なぞなぞ生成を「意味的新規性」「構造整合」「文化適合」の方向へ押し上げる再フィードバック型手法である。5 言語すべてで人間整合品質を改善し、文字体系や言語類型、モデル差を超えて汎用性を示した。AOF はテンプレ再利用や自己中心表現を抑え、各言語の修辞様式に寄った比喩的・解釈的生成を促す。DeepSeek R1, GPT-4o, LLaMA 3.1 で再現され、なぞなぞ以外の構成的・比喩的タスクにも有望である。

## 限界（Limitations）
### データセット範囲
BiRdQA（英語 6,614、中国語 8,751）の多肢選択型に依存しており、寓話や物語など大域的創造タスクへの一般化には限界がある。対象言語も EN/ZH/AR/JA/FR に限定され、低資源・高形態複雑言語は未検証。

### プロンプト・サンプリング
比較のためデコード条件を固定しており、プロンプト×パラメータ相互作用を抑制した可能性がある。MiniLM のコサイン類似は意味的新規性を捉えるが、形態差・慣用差に由来する微細な重複は見逃しうる。

### ファインチューニング設定
四択形式は構造流暢性を高める一方、明確性重視に偏る可能性がある。今後は解答者正答率や難易度校正など下流指標の評価が必要。

### 評価制約
人手評価は母語話者/熟達者で文化妥当性は確保したが、サンプル数・評価者間一致分析は資源制約がある。RiddleScore は有用な代理指標だが、記憶定着、興味持続、解く難しさなど長期的側面は直接測れない。

## 倫理声明（Ethics Statement）
### 言語公平性と文化表象
5 言語を対象にしたが、インターネット由来コーパスは少数文化の慣用・比喩を取りこぼす可能性がある。英語偏重や他言語でのパターン不足のリスクが残る。

### 創作帰属と AI 著作性
生成結果は既知なぞなぞに近似しうる。AOF は高類似候補を棄却してこのリスクを下げるが、商用利用時には追加の独自性検証が必要。実装補助・文書表現改善に LLM を用いたが、方法論・分析・最終改訂は著者が実施したと記載される。

### データプライバシーと責任ある微調整
使用データは匿名で個人識別情報を含まず、一般知識比喩中心。ファインチューニングは OpenAI API 規約・トークン制限・安全要件に従ったとされる。

### 人手評価と指標倫理
評価者ブラインド化と標準ルーブリックでバイアスを抑制。RiddleScore は創造性・流暢性・文化適合の代理として有効だが、関与度・記憶性・解答難易度は直接評価対象外。

### 悪用リスクと解釈可能性
なぞなぞ生成自体は低リスクだが、創造的曖昧性が誤情報拡散や文化的操作に使われる余地はある。高リスク領域での利用には人手レビューと解釈可能性管理が必要。

Abstract
Language models are increasingly tested on multilingual creativity, demanding
culturally grounded, abstract generations. Standard prompting methods often pro-
duce repetitive or shallow outputs. We introduce Adaptive Originality Filtering
(AOF), a prompting strategy that enforces novelty and cultural fidelity via semantic
rejection. To assess quality, we propose RiddleScore, a metric combining nov-
elty, diversity, fluency, and answer alignment. AOF improves Distinct-2 (0.915 in
Japanese), reduces Self-BLEU (0.177), and raises RiddleScore (up to +57.1% in
Arabic). Human evaluations confirm fluency, creativity, and cultural fit gains. How-
ever, improvements vary: Arabic shows greater RiddleScore gains than Distinct-2;
Japanese sees similar changes. Though focused on riddles, our method may apply
to broader creative tasks. Overall, semantic filtering with composite evaluation
offers a lightweight path to culturally rich generation—without fine-tuning.
1 Introduction
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) across
a spectrum of applications, yet their generative abilities in creative, multilingual contexts remain
underexplored and underperforming (Zhang and Wan, 2025; Ismayilzada et al., 2024). Tasks like
riddle generation pose a unique challenge: success hinges not only on linguistic fluency but also on
metaphorical abstraction, cultural resonance, and semantic ambiguity—all of which are frequently
underrepresented in LLM training corpora (Sejnowski, 2023; Pawar et al., 2024). As LLMs are
increasingly integrated into global educational and creative platforms, their limitations in culturally
grounded generation constrain both inclusivity and expressive potential(Bulathwela et al., 2024;
Spennemann, 2023).
Riddles, with their blend of metaphor, misdirection, and context-specific symbolism, provide a
compelling benchmark for evaluating multilingual creativity in NLP. However, existing prompting
strategies—zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought—often yield formulaic outputs or mistransla-
tions, especially in semantically distant or morphologically rich languages (Wei et al., 2023a; Brown
et al., 2020b). Current evaluation metrics such as BLEU, perplexity, or BERTScore are ill-equipped
to assess riddle-specific traits like structural novelty, literary device density, or cultural fit (Sellam
et al., 2020a; Dufter, 2021a).
To bridge these gaps, we propose Adaptive Originality Filtering (AOF), a prompting framework
that enforces semantic novelty and lexical diversity through a cosine similarity-based rejection
mechanism. Unlike typical generation strategies, AOF injects external control into the decoding loop,
filtering out redundant or culturally dissonant outputs to elicit more original and resonant generations.
Complementing AOF, we introduce RiddleScore, a composite evaluation metric that captures four
dimensions central to high-quality riddles: Novelty, Diversity, Fluency, and Semantic Alignment.

9 Fine-Tuned AOF Riddle Comparison to Real World
Across all five languages, fine-tuned AOF riddles diverge meaningfully from real-world counterparts
by trading formulaic structure for richer metaphor, lexical inventiveness, and cultural depth. Tra-
ditional riddles often rely on binary opposites, rhymes, or phonological puns (Gentner, 1983; An,
2023), whereas AOF generations favor conceptual blending (Fauconnier and Turner, 2002), indirect
metaphor (Lakoff and Johnson, 1980), and cross-domain abstraction (Tan et al., 2016).
EN and FR AOF riddles employ echo, shadow, or depth metaphors, including rhythmic phrasings
that support recall and poeticity (Encyclopædia Britannica, 2025). For instance, the EN riddle of
Table 13, Row 1—“I mirror your thoughts, but never speak”—explores selfhood through contrastive
metaphor, absent in real-world riddles that prefer rhyming antonyms like “shadow/light.” FR AOF
riddles follow suit, abandoning “Qu’est-ce qui” templates for ellipsis-like phrasing.
In ZH and JA, AOF outputs evoke script-specific strategies like radical-based inference and spatial
contradiction. The ZH riddle“ 千言万语藏心怀 ”(Row 2) reveals “ 信” (message/trust) through
poetic indirection, while the JA riddle “屋根にはいるのに、家にいないものは何？” juxtaposes
kanji structure and conceptual space (Sun, 2006; An, 2023).
In AR, fine-tuned riddles pivot from root-based puns to symbolic layering, favoring poetic contrasts
over mechanical symmetry. As shown in Figure 6, Row 1, metaphors like “a wind that enters but
is never welcomed” evoke hospitality norms and classical desert imagery (Al-Khatib, 1988; Antar,
2023; Liu et al., 2022b). For full comparisons and linguistic analysis, see Appendix C.
10 Conclusion
This paper introduces adaptive originality filtering (AOF), a re-feedback method for improving multi-
lingual riddle generation, pushing models towards semantically new, structurally well-formed, yet
culturally embedded, outputs. For five typologically distinct languages, AOF systematically improves
human-aligned quality measured by RiddleScore for all five confirming the approach’s universal
applicability, regardless of script, form, or model design. These advantages are a byproduct of AOF’s
design: AOF discourages revisioning of templates, discourages egocentral phrasing, and trends toward
metaphoric, interpretative styles typical for every language’s rhetorical styles. Optimized variants
of AOF, besides being better than pretrained generations, by and large are comparable to real-world
puzzles by metaphoric richness, especially in very oralistically and visualistically inclined languages
Arabic and Chinese. Additionally, AOF generalizes across LLMs, from DeepSeek R1 to GPT-4o
and from LLaMA 3.1, in manifesting strong performance across a diversity of generation styles, as
well as pretraining corpora. Apart from riddles, this work also suggests that prompting strategies
with rejection-based filtering can guide LLMs towards culturally and cognitively compatible results,
especially for compositional and figurative tasks.

Limitations
Dataset Scope
We limit our experiment to the BiRdQA corpus, comprised of 6,614 English and 8,751 Chinese
multi-choice riddles. Though genre-various, its figurative concentration limits generalizability
to larger creative tasks (e.g., allegory or storytelling). Our five-lingual evaluation extends over
EN–ZH–AR–JA–FR, but omits lower-resource or more-morphologically challenging languages like
Finnish or Swahili.
Prompting and Sampling
We uniformly set decoding hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, number of tokens) to allow for
comparison, but possibly suppress interactions between prompts and parameters. Filtering by MiniLM
in AOF targets semantic novelty, but cosine similarity may overlook certain subtle redundancies,
especially where languages are morphologically diverse or idiomatic.
Fine-Tuning Setup
Our GPT -4o fine-tuning uses BiRdQA’s multiple-choice setup, boosting structural fluency but
potentially biasing toward riddles that privilege explicit clarity over conscious ambiguity. While
stylistic refinement shows up by metrics such as Self -BLEU, Distinct-2 and RiddleScore, more
detailed downstream measurements such as solver accuracy and difficulty calibration are left to future
research.
Evaluation Constraints
Human judgments were made by native or proficient speakers from five languages employing standard
rubrics. This guarantees cultural anchoring but sample size and analysis by inter-annotator agreement
were restricted by resources. To evaluate creativity, fluency, and cultural fit, RiddleScore, tested
against these ratings, yields an interpretable proxy, albeit a proxy that doesn’t register longer-term
aspects like memorability, interest, or difficulty to solve.
Ethics Statement
Language Equity and Cultural Representation
This research assesses riddle-making within five languages, including English, Chinese, Japanese,
Arabic, and French, selected to be typologically diverse and with resources to draw from. Although
this gives a wide cultural span, the dataset and prompts come from internet-based corpora and so might
not capture perfectly idiomatic richness from less represented populations. Certain metaphorical
or rhetorical patterns might be overly represented within English or less developed within other
languages even with our balancing qualitative with quantitative assessment.
Creative Attribution and AI Authorship
Procedurally generated riddles may resemble publicly known riddles from folk sources or online
corpora. As described in Sections 3–4, Adaptive Originality Filtering (AOF) mitigates this risk by
rejecting outputs with high semantic similarity to reference data. Nonetheless, we caution against
deploying outputs in commercial settings without additional originality verification. AI assistants
(e.g., ChatGPT) were also used to support code development and manuscript preparation. During
implementation, LLMs aided in debugging and optimizing evaluation scripts (e.g., for RiddleScore
and Distinct-2). In writing, AI was used for linguistic refinement, including phrasing, transitions, and
caption clarity. All methodological contributions, analysis, and final revisions were conducted by the
authors.

Data Privacy and Responsible Fine-Tuning
These data have no personally identifiable information (PII). The riddles are anonymized and cast as
general-knowledge metaphors. The fine-tuning followed OpenAI’s API regulations, token constraints,
and safety limits, and never involved user-submitted or private material.
Human Evaluation and Metric Ethics
Human ratings were made by native or expert speakers with standardized rubrics, allowing for
culturally sensitive evaluations. Model IDs were blinded to help decrease bias. RiddleScore, tested
against these human ratings, provides a formalized proxy to creativity, fluency, and cultural fit but
doesn’t assess engagement, memorability, or difficulty for solvers.
Misuse Risks and Interpretability
While generation of riddles is a low-risk task, their creative uncertainty might be exploited to spread
misinformation or to manipulate culturally sensitive information. We advise against using them in
high-stakes educational, psychological, or legal applications without interpretability controls and
human review.

L Appendix: Fined-tuned Training and Evaluation Details
L.1 Dataset Selection and Preparation
We used the BiRdQA dataset (Zhang and Wan, 2022), a multilingual benchmark designed to test
figurative language understanding and commonsense inference. It includes 6,614 English riddles and
8,751 Chinese riddles, each paired with four answer options. Riddles were shuffled at each epoch to
prevent memorization, and no synthetic augmentation was applied.
Its linguistic diversity—spanning syntactic constructions, cultural idioms, and metaphorical phras-
ing—made BiRdQA suitable for riddle-based fine-tuning. All data were Unicode-normalized and
deduplicated, and stratified sampling ensured balanced language representation.
L.2 Training Strategy
Fine-tuning was framed as a supervised multi-class classification problem. The model selected one
correct answer out of four using cross-entropy loss. The following hyperparameters were used:
•Temperature:0.7
•Token Limit:3000
•Initial Accuracy:37–59% on development set
Training followed a three-stage pipeline: base fine-tuning, early stopping on dev performance, and
multilingual test evaluation to check generalization.
L.3 Appendix:Training Set Expansion
To improve abstraction and metaphor handling, the English and Chinese development sets were
merged into the training pool. This added examples with closely related distractors and borderline
ambiguity. After retraining, test accuracy rose to 97%.
These improvements suggest the model internalized deep riddle logic, moving beyond surface pattern

L.5 Impact of Multiple-Choice Framing
Retaining a multiple-choice structure during fine-tuning had a pronounced effect on the model’s ability
to reason through ambiguity. Unlike generative formats where any output is valid if semantically
relevant, the multiple-choice setup forced the model to:
• Distinguish between semantically similar options
• Engage in elimination-style reasoning
• Learn disambiguation strategies aligned with riddle logic
This setup simulated test-like conditions where distractors were deliberately constructed to reflect
surface-level similarity (e.g., phonetic overlaps, shared imagery, or logical decoys). The model
improved not only in accuracy but in inferential depth.
Moreover, this format likely enhanced the model’s sensitivity to misdirection—a core feature of
riddles—by requiring it to reject reasonable but incorrect answers. We observed that this effect
carried over to open-ended generation: the model became more likely to embed internal contradiction
or layered metaphor, hallmarks of real-world riddles.
In sum, multiple-choice framing served both as a task constraint and as a pedagogical scaffold,
encouraging the model to develop strategies beyond rote keyword matching.
M Appendix: AOF Prompt Template and Constraints
The Adaptive Originality Filtering (AOF) prompt enforces explicit structural rules to maximize
diversity, creativity, and cultural fit. Specifically:
• Syntactic Variety:At least half of the riddles must use poetic, declarative, or metaphorical
forms. Fewer than 3 per batch may end in “What am I?”
• Answer Filtering:Outputs with generic answers (e.g., shadow, time, echo, fire, breath) are
discarded.
• Cross-Lingual Parity:Translations must preserve ambiguity or metaphor across both
languages.
• Novelty Filter:Semantic similarity to known riddles must fall below a threshold (θ= 0.75 ),

P Appendix: Human Annotation Design and Rationale
To supplement automatic evaluation, we developed a four-part human annotation rubric, presented in
Table 32 and Table 31, to assess the quality of model-generated riddles across languages. Below, we
outline the rationale and supporting research for each criterion.
Fluency.We assess fluency as the degree to which the riddle adheres to the grammar, syntax, and
idiomatic expressions of the target language. This follows standard practices in NLG evaluation
where fluency serves as a proxy for readability and linguistic naturalness (Cahill, 2009; Van der Lee
et al., 2018).
Novelty.Novelty is a measure of how creatively the riddle diverges from common or memorized
structures. Annotators are instructed to penalize riddles that resemble known examples or rote
templates. Prior work on evaluating creativity in language models emphasizes the importance of
semantic originality and variation in structure (Dang et al., 2022; van der Lee et al., 2019).
Cultural Fit.This dimension captures how well a riddle respects linguistic or cultural norms (e.g.,
appropriate metaphors, poetic forms, or idiomatic references). For multilingual riddle generation,
cultural grounding is essential (Ponti et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2023), especially when metaphoric
reasoning is tied to local symbolism or oral traditions (Lakoff and Johnson, 1980).
Answerability.Inspired by QA evaluation practices, we define answerability as the logical coher-
ence between the riddle and its answer. This aligns with the criterion of “solvability” often applied in
linguistic humor and riddle literature (Koestler, 1964; Attardo, 1994), ensuring that riddles are not
only poetic but cognitively tractable.
Scoring Procedure.Each criterion is rated on a 5-point Likert scale. Annotators were trained
using a short calibration phase with real-world riddles from the BiRdQA corpus (Zhang and Wan,
2022). Disagreements were resolved by averaging multiple ratings per item, following best practices
in subjective NLG evaluation (van der Lee et al., 2019).
